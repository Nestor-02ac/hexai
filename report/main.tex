\section{Introduction}

Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a simulation-based decision-making algorithm widely used in games. Starting from a given position, it builds a partial search tree by iteratively applying four steps: selection, expansion, simulation, and backpropagation. After a fixed number of simulations, the move corresponding to the most visited child of the root is selected.

This approach allows the algorithm to focus computational effort on the most promising lines of play, avoiding the need to explore the full game tree exhaustively.

\paragraph{The Game of Hex}

\textcolor{red}{[Insert figure]}

Hex is a two-player deterministic board game played on an $N \times N$ hexagonal grid. It was introduced by Piet Hein in 1942 and independently rediscovered by John Nash in 1948, contributing to its popularity in academic settings.

Each player is assigned a pair of opposite sides of the board:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Player 1 (Blue) aims to connect the top and bottom sides,
    \item Player 2 (Red) aims to connect the left and right sides.
\end{itemize}

Players alternately place stones on empty cells, and stones cannot be moved or removed. A player wins by forming a continuous path of adjacent stones connecting their two sides.

\medskip 

Hex has several properties that make it particularly suitable for algorithmic study: \textbf{no draws} \cite{jing2011hex,yang2025briefreviewfixedpoints}, \textbf{perfect information} and \textbf{deterministic} behavior. These properties ensure that every simulation in MCTS produces a well-defined outcome.

\medskip

Hex is challenging from an algorithmic perspective due to its large branching factor and the difficulty of evaluating positions based on global connectivity. These characteristics make exhaustive search impractical and motivate the use of simulation-based methods such as MCTS.

\medskip 

The goal of this project is to study and compare different MCTS-based approaches for Hex within a unified framework.

We first implement a classical MCTS algorithm based on the work of Cazenave and Saffidine, combining UCT and RAVE. This implementation serves as a reference baseline, allowing us to reproduce and analyze known results under controlled conditions.

We then implement a neural-network-guided MCTS following the Gumbel AlphaZero framework \cite{danihelka2022policy}, which constitutes the main focus of this work. In this setting, a learned policy and value function replace rollout-based evaluation, enabling more efficient and informed search.

\section{Literature and state of the art}

The study of Hex-playing algorithms dates back to the early days of artificial intelligence. One of the first discussions of computer approaches to board games, including Hex, appears in Shannon’s seminal 1953 work \cite{Shannon1953ComputersAA}, which laid the foundations for game-playing AI.

Early approaches to game playing relied on minimax search combined with alpha-beta pruning, which systematically explores the game tree while eliminating branches that cannot influence the final decision. While effective in games such as chess, these methods are less suitable for Hex due to the large branching factor and the difficulty of designing accurate evaluation functions.

Modern approaches to Hex have been largely shaped by Monte Carlo Tree Search methods. Cazenave and Saffidine \cite{Cazenave2010MonteCarloH} introduced a framework combining UCT with the RAVE (Rapid Action Value Estimation) heuristic, significantly improving the efficiency of search by sharing information between simulations.

Building on these ideas, Arneson, Hayward, and Henderson developed MoHex \cite{mohex_2011}, a strong Hex-playing program based on MCTS that achieved top performance, notably winning the gold medal at the 2009 Computer Olympiad. Subsequent work further enhanced this approach by incorporating learning components. In particular, MoHex-3HNN \cite{ijcai2018p523} integrates a neural network architecture with multiple outputs, including policy and value estimates, improving both move selection and position evaluation.

More recent research has explored deeper integration of neural networks and reinforcement learning. Takada et al. introduced DeepEzo \cite{takada2020reinforcement}, which uses convolutional neural networks to learn both policy and value functions, following a paradigm similar to AlphaZero.

Finally, Cazenave’s Polygames framework \cite{cazenave2020polygamesimprovedzerolearning} demonstrates that such neural-network-guided approaches can generalize across multiple games, enabling zero-shot learning and highlighting the versatility of modern MCTS-based methods.

These works illustrate the evolution from classical search techniques to hybrid approaches combining Monte Carlo Tree Search with learned evaluation, forming the foundation for the methods explored in this project.

\section{Monte Carlo Tree Search with UCT and RAVE}

We implement the UCT + RAVE algorithm described in \cite{Cazenave2010MonteCarloH}, which serves as a reference baseline in this work. Rather than detailing the full method, we focus on the aspects specific to our implementation.

\medskip

Selection is performed using the standard UCT rule:
\[
\mathrm{UCT}(s,a) = Q(s,a) + C \sqrt{\frac{\log N(s)}{N(s,a)}},
\]
combined with RAVE (AMAF) statistics. The value of an action is interpolated between the Monte Carlo estimate and the RAVE estimate using the formulation of \cite{Cazenave2010MonteCarloH}.

In our implementation, both the exploration constant $C_{\text{uct}}$ and the RAVE bias are exposed as parameters. In particular, we investigate the case $C_{\text{uct}} = 0$, where exploration is handled primarily by RAVE.

\medskip

We consider two rollout policies: purely random simulations and a simple bridge-based heuristic (Type 2). The latter enforces local responses to bridge-breaking moves, providing a lightweight form of domain knowledge.

In contrast to the original work, we deliberately restrict the rollout policy to this simplified setting and omit higher-level heuristics such as edge templates and virtual connections. This allows us to isolate the contribution of the search algorithm itself.

\medskip

AMAF statistics are updated using all moves played during both tree traversal and rollout, following the standard RAVE convention. To maintain efficiency, bridge detection is restricted to the local neighborhood of the last move.

\medskip

These simplifications reduce the strength of individual simulations but provide a cleaner setting to study the behavior of UCT + RAVE. In particular, we observe that the effectiveness of RAVE becomes less sensitive to parameter tuning when rollout quality decreases, highlighting the dependence of AMAF statistics on simulation accuracy.

\section{Neural-Network-Guided MCTS (Gumbel AlphaZero)}

\textcolor{red}{Add pretraining comment + cython comment}

In addition to the classical MCTS approach, we implement a learning-based method following the Gumbel AlphaZero framework. In this setting, the search is guided by a neural network rather than random simulations.

Given a state $s$, the network outputs a policy $\pi(a|s)$ over actions and a value $v(s) \in [-1,1]$ estimating the expected outcome. These predictions replace the rollout phase, allowing the search to rely on learned evaluations instead of stochastic playouts.

The tree policy is based on the PUCT rule:
\[
Q(s,a) + c_{\text{puct}} \, P(s,a) \, \frac{\sqrt{N(s)}}{1 + N(s,a)},
\]
where $Q(s,a)$ is the mean value of action $a$, $P(s,a)$ the prior provided by the network, and $N(s)$ and $N(s,a)$ the visit counts. This formulation balances exploitation of high-value actions with exploration guided by the learned policy.

\subsection{Gumbel MCTS}

At the root, we follow the Gumbel AlphaZero approach. Instead of relying solely on visit counts, actions are ranked using Gumbel perturbations $g(a) \sim \text{Gumbel}(0,1)$ combined with policy logits, i.e. $g(a) + \log \pi(a)$. A subset of promising moves is selected and refined through sequential halving, progressively allocating simulations to the most competitive candidates.

This mechanism improves exploration efficiency and reduces the risk of early commitment to suboptimal moves.

\subsection{Neural Network and Training}

The evaluation function is implemented as a convolutional residual network with two outputs: a policy over legal moves and a scalar value estimate. The input encodes the board state using binary feature planes representing both players and the current turn.

Training follows the standard self-play loop: games are generated using the current model and MCTS, producing samples $(s, \pi, z)$, where $\pi$ is the improved policy and $z$ the game outcome. The network is then updated to match these targets, and the process is repeated iteratively.

In contrast to standard AlphaZero, the policy target is defined as
\[
\pi'(a|s) = \mathrm{softmax}(\log \pi(a|s) + \sigma(\bar{Q}(s,a))),
\]
rather than using raw visit counts.

\medskip

While classical MCTS relies on large numbers of simulations to reduce variance, this approach shifts the burden toward learning an accurate evaluation function, enabling more efficient and informed search.

\medskip

Our implementation follows the Gumbel AlphaZero framework and applies it to the game of Hex. While neural-network-guided MCTS has been extensively studied in other domains, its application to Hex remains less explored, making this a natural extension of existing methods.

However, in contrast to the original AlphaZero and Gumbel AlphaZero frameworks, which rely purely on self-play, we initialize the network using supervised learning on data generated by a classical MCTS agent. This provides a strong prior policy and mitigates the cold-start problem, where early self-play produces low-quality training signals.

\paragraph{Implementation details.}
To improve computational efficiency, parts of the environment (in particular the board representation and game logic) are implemented in Cython. This significantly reduces the overhead of simulations and enables faster self-play data generation, which is critical for training under limited computational resources.

\section{Results and Discussion}

We evaluate the neural-network-guided MCTS (Gumbel AlphaZero) on a $7 \times 7$ Hex board. The agent uses only 16 simulations per move and is compared against the classical MCTS baseline (UCT + RAVE + bridge rollouts) using 16,000 simulations, corresponding to a $10^3$ difference in computational budget.

\medskip

The agent quickly reaches perfect performance against random play, indicating that even early in training, the learned policy captures basic structural features of the game. More importantly, performance against the classical MCTS baseline improves steadily, reaching $65$--$70\%$ win rate after 70 iterations.

\medskip

Despite using only 16 simulations per move, the trained agent outperforms a classical MCTS player using 16,000 simulations. This result highlights the fundamental difference between the two approaches:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Classical MCTS relies on a large number of simulations to estimate values through averaging,
    \item Neural MCTS leverages a learned evaluation function to guide the search more efficiently.
\end{itemize}

The neural network effectively replaces expensive rollouts by providing both a strong policy prior and a value estimate, allowing the search to focus on relevant parts of the game tree.

\medskip 

The progression over iterations shows a clear learning dynamic. Performance initially improves rapidly, then exhibits fluctuations before stabilizing and reaching its highest level. This non-monotonic behavior reflects the interplay between self-play data generation and network updates.

The use of supervised pretraining plays a key role in this process. By initializing the network with expert moves generated by classical MCTS, we avoid the cold-start problem where early self-play produces uninformative data. This significantly accelerates convergence and stabilizes training.

\subsection{Discussion}

These results demonstrate that neural-network-guided MCTS can outperform classical rollout-based approaches under a constrained simulation budget. The gain in efficiency is particularly striking, as the learned model compensates for a three-order-of-magnitude reduction in search effort.

More generally, this highlights a shift in paradigm: instead of relying on increasingly accurate simulations, modern approaches focus on learning compact representations of the game that can guide search directly.

Finally, it is important to note that our implementation does not strictly follow a pure AlphaZero setting, due to the use of supervised pretraining and reduced board size. Nevertheless, the observed performance confirms that the core principles of Gumbel AlphaZero transfer effectively to the game of Hex.

\section{Limitations and Perspectives}

Neural-network-guided MCTS introduces a significant computational overhead due to the training phase. While the resulting model enables more efficient search at inference time, this comes at the cost of generating self-play data and performing iterative optimization. This creates a trade-off between upfront computational cost and eventual performance.

\medskip

Moreover, the benefits of neural approaches are less pronounced on small board sizes. In such settings, classical MCTS with a sufficient number of simulations can already explore a large fraction of the search space, reducing the advantage of learned evaluation functions. As a result, the relative gain of neural methods increases with problem complexity.

\medskip

Finally, we also proposed an implementation for the Game of Y, but Hex belongs to a broader class of connection games with many other variants. Extending the framework to support multiple games through a unified interface would improve its generality and allow systematic evaluation of algorithmic performance across different geometries and rules.
